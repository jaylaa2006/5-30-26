I am Seba Khafre, and I want to share the oldest teaching of the Per Ankh — the House of Life. Long before there were schools, there were families gathered close, one voice passing the story to the next. This is how our ancestors carried their wisdom: not handed down, but handed across.

So tonight, try this. Open a story and take turns — one page each. When it is your child’s turn, let them carry the words, even the hard ones. When it is yours, let them hear how an elder’s voice slows at the important parts. The reading itself becomes a small act of Reciprocity — the giving and receiving that holds Maat together.

But Maat asks a little more of us than reading. It asks us to think. So when the story pauses, do not rush to the next page. Ask together:

◆  Was that fair? (Justice)
◆  Was that the truth, even when it was hard to say? (Truth)
◆  Did it bring the family back into balance? (Harmony, Balance)
◆  What would I have done, and would it have been right?

There are no wrong answers in this circle. A child who is asked “what do you think?” learns that their mind matters — and a parent who truly listens learns that wisdom can rise from small voices too. This is Maat: not a rule to obey, but a balance we keep together.

You need not be a teacher. You need only sit close, turn the page, and wonder out loud. The ancestors did not raise children in rows facing forward. They raised them in circles, facing one another.

Tonight, gather your family and open a single story.
