A few verses after dinner

Hotep, {{greeting}}.

I am Seba Khafre. Last time I asked you to read one story page each, and to pause and think in the way of Maat. Today I want to place a different book in your hands — one for the elders of the home as much as the young ones.

It is called Maat: Guiding Principles of Moral Living — more than a thousand pages, in color, gathering the principles our ancestors lived and judged their own hearts by. It is not a book to be swallowed in one sitting. It is a well to return to.

So here is the practice I would offer you. After dinner, when the plates are cleared and the house is quiet, open it and read two or three pages aloud — no more. Let one principle settle on the table like a lamp. Then ask, as we always do:

◆  Where did I see this today — in myself, in others?
◆  Where was it hard to keep?
◆  What is one small way we live it tomorrow?

Children learn Maat not by being told it, but by watching their parents weigh their own days against it. When a child sees a grown person read a principle and say “I could have done better here,” that child learns the heart can always be set right. That is the whole teaching of the scales.

Three pages. One principle. One honest question around the table. This is how a household keeps Maat — gently, nightly, together.

Bring the principles to your table tonight.
